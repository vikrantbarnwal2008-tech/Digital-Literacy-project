# 🛅 Case Study: Phishing Attack on Twitter (2020)

Phishing is a cybercrime where attackers impersonate trusted entities to deceive victims into revealing sensitive information like login credentials,
financial details, or access rights via fraudulent emails, messages, or websites. It exploits human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities.

A real-world example is the July 2020 Twitter Bitcoin scam, one of the largest phishing incidents targeting a major platform. Hackers, led by 17-year-old 
Graham Ivan Clark, used social engineering to phish Twitter employees.

## Step-by-step how it typically happens (and in this case):

** Reconnaissance **: Attackers research targets via LinkedIn or company directories.
** Bait delivery **: They send phishing emails or texts mimicking IT support (e.g., "Your VPN needs resetting—click here"). In the Twitter case,
hackers called internal tools hotline, posing as staff.
** Credential theft **: Victims enter details on fake sites; attackers use them for internal access.
** Lateral movement **: Gain admin privileges to target high-value accounts (e.g., Obama, Biden, Musk).
** Exploitation **: Hijack accounts to post Bitcoin scam tweets promising doubled payments.

## Usually targeted:
Employees at tech firms (helpdesk, IT), high-net-worth individuals, businesses with valuable data. Everyday users via mass emails, but spear-phishing hits specifics.

## Consequences:
Twitter lost $120,000+ in Bitcoin, accounts of 130 celebrities/politicians compromised, eroding trust. Company faced SEC probe, stock dip, lawsuits.
Clark was sentenced to 3 years; co-conspirators pleaded guilty. Victims endured reputational harm; globally, phishing causes $5B+ annual losses (FBI IC3).
